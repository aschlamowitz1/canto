Cantonese Tutor - Level 1: Foundation

Level 1: Introduction to Tones and Basic Greetings

Goal: Understand the concept of Cantonese tones, learn the most fundamental greetings, and practice initial pronunciation.

Lesson 1.1: The Six Tones of Cantonese

Cantonese is a tonal language, which means the same sound can have different meanings depending on the pitch contour of your voice. This is crucial for being understood! There are six main tones in standard Cantonese.

Let's use the sound "aa" (like the "a" in "father") as an example for all six tones.

  • High Flat (陰平 - yam ping): High and level. Like singing a high note and holding it.
    Example: $aa^1$ (啊) - often used as an exclamation of surprise or understanding.
  • High Rising (陰上 - yam seung): Starts mid-range and rises to high. Like asking a question in English.
    Example: $aa^2$ (啊) - similar to tone 1, but with a rising intonation.
  • Mid Flat (陰去 - yam heui): Mid-range and level.
    Example: $aa^3$ (啊) - similar to tone 1, but at a lower pitch.
  • Low Falling (陽平 - yeung ping): Starts low and falls even lower.
    Example: $aa^4$ (啊) - similar to tone 1, but with a falling intonation.
  • Low Rising (陽上 - yeung seung): Starts low and rises to mid-range. This one can be tricky for English speakers.
    Example: $aa^5$ (啊) - similar to tone 2, but at a lower starting pitch.
  • Low Flat (陽去 - yeung heui): Low and level.
    Example: $aa^6$ (啊) - similar to tone 3, but at a lower pitch.
Important Note: Don't get overwhelmed by the tones initially. We'll practice them constantly with real words. The best way to learn is by listening and imitating.

Lesson 1.2: Basic Greetings

Now let's put some tones into action with essential greetings.

  • Hello / Hi: 你好 (nei5 hou2)
    • 你 (nei5) - you (low rising tone)
    • 好 (hou2) - good (high rising tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: "nei" sounds like "nay" in English. "hou" sounds like "ho" in "hoe."
  • Good Morning: 早晨 (jou2 san4)
    • 早 (jou2) - early (high rising tone)
    • 晨 (san4) - morning (low falling tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: "jou" sounds like "joe." "san" sounds like "sun."
  • Good Night: 晚安 (maan5 on1)
    • 晚 (maan5) - evening (low rising tone)
    • 安 (on1) - peace/safe (high flat tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: "maan" sounds like "man" in "maniac." "on" sounds like "on."

Lesson 1.3: Practice Pronunciation

The best way to get these tones right is to listen to native speakers and try to mimic them. Since I can't play audio directly here, I strongly recommend you use an online Cantonese dictionary or a translator app with audio playback. Search for "Cantonese pronunciation audio" for these phrases.

Level 1: Basic Personal Pronouns and "To Be"

Goal: Learn how to say "I," "you," and "he/she/it," and introduce the concept of "to be."

Lesson 1.4: Personal Pronouns

These are fundamental for constructing even the simplest sentences.

  • I / Me: 我 (ngo5)
    Pronunciation Tip: Sounds a bit like "ngaw" (like "Gaw" with an "n" sound at the beginning, like the "ng" in "sing"). This is the low rising tone (Tone 5).
  • You (singular, informal): 你 (nei5)
    Pronunciation Tip: We already learned this! Sounds like "nay." (Tone 5).
  • He / She / It: 佢 (keoi5)
    Pronunciation Tip: Sounds like "koy" (rhymes with "boy"). This is also a low rising tone (Tone 5).

Notice a pattern with the tones for these pronouns? They all share the same tone! This can make it a little easier to remember their pitch.

Lesson 1.5: The Verb "To Be" (係 - hai6)

In Cantonese, the verb "to be" (is, am, are) is 係 (hai6).

  • 係 (hai6) - is / am / are (low flat tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: Sounds like "hai" (rhymes with "high").

Let's put this together with our pronouns.

  • I am: 我係 (ngo5 hai6)
  • You are: 你係 (nei5 hai6)
  • He/She/It is: 佢係 (keoi5 hai6)

Lesson 1.6: Simple Introductions

Now we can combine everything to form very basic sentences.

  • I am [Name]: 我係 [Name]. (ngo5 hai6 [Name])
    Example: 我係 Tom. (I am Tom.)
  • Are you [Name]?: 你係唔係 [Name]? (nei5 hai6 m4 hai6 [Name]?)

    唔係 (m4 hai6) is "is not" / "am not" / "are not." This construction "A is B, or is not B?" is a common way to ask a "yes/no" question in Cantonese. We'll dive deeper into questions later, but it's good to see it now.

    • 唔 (m4) - not (low falling tone, almost a hum)
    • 係 (hai6) - is (low flat tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: "m" is just a short hum, like saying "mmm-hmm" without the "hmm."

Lesson 1.7: Saying "Yes" and "No"

Goal: Learn the various ways to say "yes" and "no" in Cantonese, and understand their common usages.

1.7.1: "Yes" and "No" (Direct)

The simplest way to answer "yes" or "no" to a "to be" question (like "Are you...?") is to use the verb 係 (hai6) itself, or its negation 唔係 (m4 hai6).

  • Yes: 係 (hai6) - (low flat tone)
    Pronunciation Tip: Sounds like "hai" (rhymes with "high"). This is the same word as "is/am/are."
  • No: 唔係 (m4 hai6) - (low falling tone for 唔, low flat tone for 係)
    Pronunciation Tip: "m" is a hum, "hai" as before. This is the same as "is not/am not/are not."

Example Dialogue:

A: 你係 Peter 嗎？ (nei5 hai6 Peter ma1?) - Are you Peter?

B: 係。 (hai6) - Yes, I am.

C: 唔係。 (m4 hai6) - No, I'm not.

*(Note: 嗎 (ma1) is a common question particle, which we'll learn more about later. For now, focus on the answers.)*

1.7.2: Softer Affirmations/Negations (呀 - aa3)

Adding the particle 呀 (aa3) makes the answer sound softer, more polite, or confirms understanding.

  • Yes (softer/confirming): 係呀 (hai6 aa3) - (low flat for 係, mid flat for 呀)
    Pronunciation Tip: "aa" here is like the "a" in "father," with a mid-level tone.
  • No (softer negation): 唔係呀 (m4 hai6 aa3) - (low falling for 唔, low flat for 係, mid flat for 呀)

Example Dialogue:

A: 佢係 Lily 呀？ (keoi5 hai6 Lily aa3?) - Is she Lily?

B: 係呀。 (hai6 aa3) - Yes, she is. (Confirming)

C: 唔係呀。 (m4 hai6 aa3) - No, she isn't. (A bit softer than just 唔係)

1.7.3: "Yes" (Emphatic/Natural) - 係嘅 (hai6 ge2)

Adding 嘅 (ge2) can make "yes" sound more natural, or emphatic, often implying "that's right" or "it is indeed."

  • Yes (emphatic/natural): 係嘅 (hai6 ge2) - (low flat for 係, high rising for 嘅)
    Pronunciation Tip: "ge" sounds like "geh" in "get."

Example Dialogue:

A: 你係學生嚟㗎？ (nei5 hai6 hok6 saang1 lai4 ga3?) - Are you a student?

B: 係嘅。 (hai6 ge2) - Yes, I am. (Natural, affirmative response)

1.7.4: "Good/Okay" (可以 - ho2 yi5)

While not a direct "yes" or "no" to a "to be" question, "good" or "okay" can often serve as an affirmative answer to proposals or suggestions.

  • Okay / All right: 可以 (ho2 yi5) - (high rising for 可, low rising for 以)
    Pronunciation Tip: "ho" as in "hoe", "yi" as in "yee".

Example Dialogue:

A: 我哋而家去食飯？ (ngo5 dei6 yi4 gaa1 heoi3 sik6 faan6?) - Shall we go eat now?

B: 可以。 (ho2 yi5) - Okay / All right.
